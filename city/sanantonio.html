🗿 Oddities · 8

  • Big Cowboy Boots (North Star Mall)North San Antonio (Loop 410 / San Pedro Avenue)
    A pair of giant cowboy boots, billed as the world's largest, marking the entrance to North Star Mall off Loop 410.
  • Bracken Cave PreserveGarden Ridge / southern Comal County (north of San Antonio)
    The summer home of the world's largest bat colony - up to 20 million Mexican free-tailed bats spiraling out of a sinkhole at dusk.
  • Buckhorn Saloon & MuseumDowntown (Houston Street)
    An 1881 saloon crammed floor-to-ceiling with horns, taxidermy and oddities, where the founder once traded drinks for antlers.
  • Donkey Lady BridgeFar South Side / Applewhite
    A 1917 bridge on the old Applewhite Road alignment, the focus of San Antonio's enduring 'Donkey Lady' ghost legend.
  • Haunted Railroad Tracks (Ghost Tracks)South Side / Shane Road
    A South Side railroad crossing where parked cars seem to roll uphill off the tracks - blamed on ghost children.
  • The Berlin BearHemisfair Park (Downtown)
    A bronze Berlin bear gifted by West Berlin for HemisFair '68, still standing guard by the Victorian Halff House.
  • Tom Slick Community Park (Nessie statue)West San Antonio (TX-151 / Westover Hills)
    A city park named for San Antonio's eccentric millionaire Yeti-and-Nessie hunter, with a metal Loch Ness Monster statue rising from its quarry pond.
  • Woman Hollering CreekEast Bexar County / near Schertz
    A real creek whose name loosely translates the 'La Llorona' legend, crossed by Interstate 10 east of San Antonio.

📜 Deep history · 8

  • Casa Navarro State Historic SiteDowntown
    Home of one of only two native-born Texans to sign the Declaration
  • Espada AqueductSouth Side
    Franciscan stone aqueduct built in 1731, still carrying water today
  • Hot Wells Hotel and Spa RuinsSouth Side / Hot Wells
    Stabilized ruins of a Victorian sulfur-springs resort that once drew bathers, film studios and high society.
  • Kiddie ParkBrackenridge Park / San Antonio Zoo (Midtown)
    Billed as America's oldest children's amusement park, with a hand-carved 1918 carousel - relocated in 2019 to the San Antonio Zoo.
  • Menger HotelDowntown
    Where Teddy Roosevelt drank and recruited his Rough Riders in 1898
  • Stinson Municipal AirportSouth Side
    Second-oldest US airport in continuous operation, opened by women in 1915
  • The Quadrangle at Fort Sam HoustonFort Sam Houston (JBSA), northeast San Antonio
    An 1876 windowless military quadrangle that briefly held Geronimo - now an open courtyard roamed by tame deer, peacocks and rabbits.
  • Tower Life BuildingDowntown
    Gothic grotesques on the tallest eight-sided skyscraper in the US

🌿 Green & river · 6

  • Chris Park (CHRISpark)Southtown / King William (Camp Street)
    A one-acre private-public memorial garden a philanthropist built for her late son, with journal-inscribed limestone benches and a hidden LED constellation.
  • Japanese Tea GardenBrackenridge Park
    A sunken Japanese garden carved into an abandoned limestone quarry, with a 60-foot waterfall and koi ponds.
  • McNutt Sculpture GardenDowntown (River Walk, Market Street)
    A free riverside courtyard garden of bronze cowboys, longhorns and cougars beside the Briscoe Western Art Museum.
  • MirafloresBrackenridge / Broadway
    Dr. Aureliano Urrutia's lost Mexican garden of art, scattered with faux bois sculptures and Talavera-tiled gates.
  • San Antonio Botanical GardenBroadway Corridor
    Underground greenhouse sunk into one of the city's highest points
  • San Pedro Springs ParkMidtown
    Texas's oldest public park, on the spring where San Antonio was founded

🏛️ Museums · 4

  • McNay Art MuseumAlamo Heights
    Texas's first modern art museum, bequeathed inside a mansion on 23 acres
  • San Antonio Museum of ArtPearl
    World-class Latin American collection inside an 1886 River Walk brewery
  • U.S. Army Medical Department Museum (AMEDD Museum)Fort Sam Houston (JBSA), northeast San Antonio
    A military-medicine museum of battlefield ambulances, field hospitals and POW artifacts on the Army's medical home base.
  • Witte MuseumBrackenridge Park / Broadway
    Keeps the cedar bench an airman named Johnny Cash knife-carved "Johnny Loves Vivian" into in 1951.

🏰 Follies · 3

  • Dionicio Rodriguez Faux Bois Canopied Table & BenchBrackenridge Park
    A concrete picnic table under a fake "thatched" palapa roof - both sculpted entirely from cement.
  • Dionicio Rodriguez Faux Bois FootbridgeBrackenridge Park
    A concrete footbridge so convincingly "wooden" that woodpeckers peck at its bark hunting for insects.
  • Dionicio Rodriguez Faux Bois Trolley StopAlamo Heights
    A bus shelter of "petrified wood" that is really hand-sculpted concrete - the city's most accessible artwork.

🛕 Faith & ritual · 3

  • Mission ConcepcionMission District
    Solar-aligned door floods the interior on the Feast of the Assumption yearly
  • Mission San JoséMission District
    The Queen of the Missions, its Rose Window carved for a love lost at sea
  • San Fernando CathedralDowntown
    One of the oldest US cathedrals; Santa Anna flew no-quarter from its tower

🎸 Music lore · 2

  • Arneson River TheatreLa Villita
    WPA outdoor stage where the river itself divides performers from their crowd
  • Guadalupe Cultural Arts CenterWest Side
    Home of the world's largest Tejano conjunto festival and its Hall of Fame

🏟️ Stadiums · 1

  • Alamo StadiumMonte Vista
    WPA limestone amphitheatre in a quarry — the 'Rock Pile' since 1940

🏙️ Art Deco · 1

  • Aztec TheaterDowntown
    Mesoamerican revival palace with Montezuma meeting Cortés on the fire screen

🪦 Death & burial · 1

  • Grave of Pat the HorseFort Sam Houston (JBSA), northeast San Antonio
    A marble grave with four embedded horseshoes for an Army cavalry horse who got a full military funeral with Taps.

🎨 Street art · 1

  • La Veladora of Our Lady of Guadalupe MosaicWest Side (Guadalupe Street)
    A monumental votive-candle (veladora) mosaic of the Virgin of Guadalupe, a tiled beacon on the West Side's Guadalupe Theater.

  • Majestic TheatreDowntown
    First air-conditioned Texas theatre; stars placed by National Geographic
